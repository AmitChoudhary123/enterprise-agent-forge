# Executive Point of View: Agentic AI Needs an Operating Model, Not Just a Framework

Agentic AI matters now because enterprises are moving from AI that advises to AI that acts. The risk profile changes the moment an AI system can call tools, trigger workflows, recommend financial decisions, or interact with customers. The question is no longer only whether the model is accurate. The question is whether the enterprise can govern the action.

## What most companies are missing

Most organizations are still treating agents as technology pilots. They evaluate frameworks, prompt patterns, and model quality, but they underinvest in the operating model around the agent:

- Who owns the agent's business outcome?
- Which actions require approval?
- What evidence is retained after a decision?
- How are incidents escalated?
- Which controls are automated and which remain human-owned?

Without these answers, agentic AI becomes unmanaged process automation with a probabilistic interface.

## Required operating model

Enterprise agents need a governance model that is embedded in execution, not reviewed after the fact. The minimum operating model should include:

- Business owner for every agent workflow
- Risk tiering by action type, customer impact, and financial exposure
- Approval gates for high-risk or high-value actions
- Audit trail for plans, tool calls, evidence, and policy decisions
- Incident response path for model, tool, or workflow failure
- Periodic review of agent outcomes, exceptions, and overrides

The governance should be lightweight, but it must be real.

## Common failure patterns

- Teams optimize for autonomy before accountability
- Tool calls are added faster than policy controls
- Pilots use synthetic demos that hide operational complexity
- Human review is bolted on manually instead of designed into the workflow
- Leaders approve scale-up without seeing traceability, incident paths, or rollback plans

These failures are predictable. They happen when agent design is separated from enterprise delivery discipline.

## A practical 12-month leadership agenda

1. Identify 5-8 agent workflows with clear business owners and measurable outcomes.
2. Classify each workflow by risk, value, reversibility, and customer impact.
3. Build a reusable approval and audit pattern before scaling tools.
4. Create a standard agent run report for executives, risk teams, and engineering.
5. Pilot low-risk workflows first, but design the control model for high-risk workflows from day one.
6. Add observability for tool calls, cost, latency, exceptions, and human overrides.
7. Establish a monthly agent governance review to inspect outcomes, failures, and control breaches.
8. Move from isolated pilots to a platform pattern only after the operating model is proven.

The winning enterprises will not be the ones with the most autonomous agents. They will be the ones that make agentic AI operationally trustworthy.
